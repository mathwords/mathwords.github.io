    • We proceed as follows. x∈f−1(f(A)) ⇒ f(x)∈f(A) ⇒ ∃x′∈A:f(x′)=f(x). But from the injectivity of f we have f(x′)=f(x)⇒x′=x. It follows that x∈A. Hence, f−1(f(A))⊆A.

  • Suppose that a is False. Then ∃x1,∃x2∈X, such that (x1≠x2)∧(f(x1)=f(x2)). Now consider the set {x1}⊂X. We find that (x2∉{x1})∧(x2∈f−1(f({x1}))). In other words, we have shown that ∃A={x1}⊂X such that f−1(f(A))≠A. We therefore have, ¬a⇒¬b, which is the same as b⇒a.

  • Suppose that a is True. We will now examine c. We proceed as follows.

    • f(x)∈f(A∩B) ⇒ ∃x′∈A∩B:f(x′)=f(x). But from the injectivity of f we have f(x′)=f(x)⇒x′=x. It follows that x∈A∩B which in turn implies that (x∈A)∧(x∈B) ⇒ (f(x)∈f(A))∧(f(x)∈f(B)) ⇒ f(x)∈f(A)∩f(B). Hence, f(A∩B)⊆f(A)∩f(B).

    • On the other hand, f(x)∈f(A)∩f(B) ⇒ ∃x1∈A:f(x1)=f(x), and ∃x2∈B:f(x2)=f(x). But from the injectivity of f we have f(x1)=f(x)⇒x1=x, and likewise f(x2)=f(x)⇒x2=x. We therefore have (x∈A)∧(x∈B). It follows that x∈A∩B, and consequently f(x)∈f(A∩B). Hence, f(A)∩f(B)⊆f(A∩B).

  • Proof that the given function is injective: Suppose that A and B are subsets of X, such that A≠B. With no loss of generality we can then assume that A⊈B. Then ∃a∈A:a∉B. It follows that (a,1)∈χA, but (a,1)∉χB. We have shown that A≠B⇒χA≠χB. Therefore the given function is injective.

  • Proof that the given function is surjective: ∀χK∈{0,1}X, we have K⊆X. Hence, K∈P(X). Every χK∈{0,1}X therefore has a preimage K∈P(X).
