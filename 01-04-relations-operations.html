I.4 Relations and Operations

 

Exercise 1

We will prove that f∗:X/∼→Y/∼˙ defined by f∗([x])=[f(x)] is the function that fulfils the required conditions.

  • Indeed, (pY∘f)(x) = (pY(f(x))) = [f(x)]. Whereas, (f∗∘pX)(x) = f∗(pX(x)) = f∗([x]) = [f(x)]. Thus, f∗∘pX=pY∘f and consequently the given diagram is commutative.
  • Now suppose that g is another function that fulfils the required condition, then we require that g∘pX=pY∘f, that is, g(pX(x))=[f(x)]. But pX(x)=[x], and [f(x)]= f∗([x]). Hence, g([x])= f∗([x]). We have proved this result for all pX(x)∈X/∼. But since pX is surjective, it follows that every [x]∈X/∼ can be written as pX(x) for some x∈X. We therefore have, g=f∗ so that f∗ is unique.

 

Exercise 2

Let X be a set with at least two elements and X:=P(X)∖{X} with the inclusion order. We will prove that the identity function X→X,A↦A is not bounded. Suppose to the contrary that the given function is bounded. Then ∃B∈X,∀A∈X:A⊆B. But then we have x∈X ⇒ {x}∈X ⇒ {x}⊆B ⇒ x∈B. That is, X⊆B. Whereas, B∈P(X) ⇒ B⊆X. It follows that B=X, and hence X∈X, which is a contradiction. The original assumption is False, so that ∀B∈X,∃A∈X:A⊈B. The given function is not bounded.

 

Exercise 3

Suppose that a,b∈X:a≠b. Now consider f,g∈Funct(X,X) defined by f(x)=a and g(x)=b. We now have (f∘g)(x)=a, whereas (g∘f)(x)=b.

 

Exercise 4

Let ⊛ be an anticommutative operation on X. Then we have, from the given conditions:

  1. x=y⇒x⊛y=r
  2. (x⊛y)⊛(y⊛x)=r⇒x=y

Let X be a set that has more than one element. Then, in addition to r (the right identity), ∃a∈X:a≠r.

Now, if ⊛ is commutative, or if r is also a left identity, then we have in particular: r⊛a=a⊛r. It follows from condition (1) that (r⊛a)⊛(a⊛r)=r. But then it follows from condition (2) that r=a, which is a contradiction. Hence, ⊛ cannot be commutative.

 

Exercise 5

Let ⊛ and ⊚ be anticommutative operations on X and Y respectively. Further, f:X→Y satisfies:

  1. f(rX)=rY
  2. f(x⊛y)=f(x)⊚f(y),   x,y∈X.

a) Consider x∼y  :⟺ f(x⊛y)=rY.

  • The relation is reflexive because f(x⊛x) =f(rX) =rY.
  • Next, we assume that f(x⊛y)=rY. It follows that f(x)⊚f(y)=rY. From the anticommutativity of ⊚, we have f(x)=f(y). Whereas, f(y⊛x) =f(y)⊚f(x)=rY. Where the last step follows from the anticommutativity of ⊚ and the fact that f(x)=f(y). Hence ∼ is symmetric.
  • Suppose that x∼y  and y∼z. Then, it follows as above that f(x)=f(y) and f(y)=f(z). Thus, f(x)=f(z). From this it follows that f(x⊛z) =f(x)⊚f(z) =rY. Hence, ∼ is transitive.

Therefore, ∼ is an equivalence relation.

b) Consider the mapping f~:X/∼→Y, [x]↦f(x). From part (a), recall that we proved that x∼y⇔f(x)=f(y). It follows that [x]=[y]⇔f(x)=f(y). Therefore, f~ is well defined and injective. If f is surjective, then y∈Y⇒y=f(x). But f(x)=f~([x]), so that f~ is also surjective, and hence bijective.

 

Exercise 9

  • A∈A⇒A⊆⋃A. Hence, ⋃A is an upper bound of A. Now, suppose B is any upper bound of A, then x∉B ⇒ ∀A∈A:x∉A ⇒ x∉⋃A. Hence, ⋃A⊆B. Therefore, ⋃A is the least upper bound of A.
  • A∈A⇒⋂A⊆A. Hence, ⋂A is a lower bound of A. Now, suppose B is any lower bound of A, then x∈B ⇒ ∀A∈A:x∈A ⇒ x∈⋂A. Hence, B⊆⋂A. Therefore, ⋂A is the greatest lower bound of A.
