I.3 Functions

 

Exercise 1 (Proof of Proposition 3.6)

Proposition 3.6 Let f:X→Y and g:Y→V be bijective. Then g∘f:X→V is bijective and

(g∘f)−1=f−1∘g−1

Proof

  • Suppose that f and g are both injective. g(f(x1))=g(f(x2)) ⟹ f(x1)=f(x2) ⟹ x1=x2. First implication is true because g is injective and the second implication is true because f is injective. Hence injectivity of f and g implies injectivity of g∘f.
  • Suppose that f and g are both surjective. Since g is surjective: ∀v∈V,∃y∈Y, such that g(y)=v. And, since f is surjective: ∀y∈Y,∃x∈X, such that f(x)=y. Consequently, ∀v∈V,∃x∈X, such that g(f(x))=v. Hence surjectivity of f and g implies surjectivity of g∘f.
  • (g∘f)∘(f−1∘g−1) = ((g∘f)∘f−1)∘g−1 = (g∘(f∘f−1))∘g−1 = (g∘idY)∘g−1 = g∘g−1 = idV. Also, (f−1∘g−1)∘(g∘f) = ((f−1∘g−1)∘g)∘f = (f−1∘(g−1∘g))∘f = (f−1∘idY)∘f = f−1∘f = idX. It follows from the above results that f−1∘g−1 is the inverse of g∘f (and by Proposition 3.5, the inverse is unique).

 

Exercise 3

a) See Exercise 1.

b) We need to prove both sides of the implication.

  • (⇒) Suppose that f is injective. We construct h:Y→X as follows.

    • From the injectivity of f it follows that ∀y∈f(X), ∃!x∈X, such that y=f(x). We begin our construction of h by first defining h:f(X)→X by the mapping f(x)↦x.
    • Next, define h:Y∖f(X)→X by the mapping y↦x1, where x1 is any element of X.

    With these two steps, we have constructed h:Y→X such that (h∘f)(x)=h(f(x))=x. Hence, h∘f=idX.

  • (⇐) Suppose that (h∘f=idX) ∧ ¬(f is injective). Then ∃x1,∃x2∈X, such that (x1≠x2)∧(f(x1)=f(x2)). But since h is a function, we have: f(x1)=f(x2)⇒h(f(x1))=h(f(x2)). But given that h∘f=idX, we have h(f(x1))=h(f(x2))⇒x1=x2. This contradiction establishes that the original assumption is false. Therefore, (h∘f=idX) ⇒ (f is injective).

c) Once again, we need to prove both sides of the implication.

  • (⇒) Suppose that f is surjective. We construct h:Y→X as follows. From the surjectivity of f it follows that ∀y∈Y, ∃x∈X, such that y=f(x). If for each y, we select any one such x, then every y∈Y can be written in the form f(x). Now, define h:Y→X by the mapping f(x)↦x. Based on this definition of h, we now have: h(y)=h(f(x))=x. We can now evaluate f∘h as follows: (f∘h)(y) =f(h(y)) = f(x)=y. Therefore, f∘h=idY.
  • (⇐) Suppose that (f∘h=idY) ∧ ¬(f is surjective). Then ∃y∈Y,∀x∈X:(f(x)≠y). But, given that (f∘h=idY), we have ∀y∈Y:f(h(y))=y. But h(y)∈X. So we now have ∀y∈Y,∃x∈X:(f(x)=y). This contradiction establishes that the original assumption is false. Therefore, (f∘h=idY) ⇒ (f is surjective).
